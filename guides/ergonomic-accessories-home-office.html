Ergonomic Accessories for Home Office

Footrests, keyboard trays, wrist pads, monitor arms — the small upgrades that make a big difference.

You don't need a $3,000 chair or a motorized sit-stand desk to transform your workspace. Most of the time, it's the small stuff — the accessories you add to your existing setup — that make the biggest difference between finishing the workday pain-free and rolling your shoulders for the twentieth time at 3 p.m.

Think about it: you probably already have a desk and a chair. Maybe you even have a decent monitor. But if your screen is sitting on the stock stand it shipped with, your wrists are resting on the hard edge of your desk, and your feet are dangling because your chair's too high — yeah, you're going to feel that by Thursday.

Ergonomic accessories bridge the gap between the setup you have and the setup you need. They're the low-hanging fruit of workspace health. And the best part? Most of them cost less than a nice dinner out.

This guide breaks down every accessory that's worth your money — what it does, who needs it, how much to spend, and which specific products to buy. I've organized it so you can read straight through or jump to the section that's bothering you right now.

Monitor Arms — The #1 Upgrade You Can Make

If you can only buy one ergonomic accessory this year, make it a monitor arm. Full stop.

Here's why: the stand that came with your monitor was designed to get the box checked, not to put the screen at eye level. Most stock stands sit the screen so low you have to tilt your head down to see it. Do that for eight hours a day, and your neck is going to let you know about it.

A monitor arm frees up desk space (no more giant plastic base), lets you position the screen exactly where it needs to be — at eye level, an arm's length away — and makes it trivial to switch between sitting and standing without hunting for a ream of paper to prop things up.

The budget pick: Amazon Basics Monitor Arm ($22)

The Amazon Basics Single Monitor Arm is the best value in ergonomics, full stop. It's a rebadge of a proven design (it shares DNA with the much more expensive Ergotron LX), and at $22, it's almost suspiciously cheap for what it does. It supports monitors up to 32 inches and about 22 pounds, has full articulation (height, tilt, swivel, rotation), and clamps to your desk or mounts through a grommet hole. The gas spring is smooth enough for most users. The only catch? The cable management channels are mediocre, and the plastic joints don't feel quite as premium as pricier options. But if you're on a tight budget, this is the arm to get.

The premium pick: Ergotron LX ($139)

The Ergotron LX is the gold standard for a reason. It's built like a tank — all aluminum, no plastic nonsense. The gas spring is butter-smooth, holding position exactly where you leave it without any drift. It supports up to 20 pounds (most monitors 24 inches and under are fine, though some 27-inch ultrawides push the limit), and the cable management is genuinely useful, not an afterthought. The LX has been the go-to arm for IT departments and ergonomic consultants for over a decade. The price stings at $139, but this is a buy-it-for-life product. I've seen LXs in active use for over a decade without any sag or failure.

Quick buying advice: Make sure your desk has a solid edge for clamping — glass tops and thin particleboard desks can be problematic. Measure your monitor's VESA pattern (most modern monitors are 100×100mm, but it's worth checking before you buy). And if you're running dual monitors, buy two single arms rather than a dual-arm setup — it gives you more flexibility to position each screen independently.

Footrests — Your Feet Aren't Supposed to Dangle

Here's a scenario I see all the time: someone buys an ergonomic chair, adjusts the seat height so their thighs are parallel to the floor — and now their feet don't touch the ground. They sit there with their heels hooked on the chair legs or their toes barely grazing the carpet. It's uncomfortable, but they figure it's fine.

It's not fine.

When your feet aren't supported, your hamstrings tighten, your pelvis tilts backward, and you end up slouching. The whole point of that expensive chair adjustment is lost because there's nothing under your feet. A footrest is the fix.

Who needs one? Anyone who's over about 5'8" in a standard office chair. Anyone using a thick cushion or an aftermarket seat pad. Anyone with a standing-height desk who can't lower it all the way. And honestly, even if your feet do touch the floor, a footrest with a tilt function can make a noticeable difference in lower back comfort by encouraging micro-movements and keeping your legs from going completely static.
