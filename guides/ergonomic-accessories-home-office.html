Ergonomic Accessories for Home Office

Footrests, keyboard trays, wrist pads, monitor arms — the small upgrades that make a big difference.

You don't need a $3,000 chair or a motorized sit-stand desk to transform your workspace. Most of the time, it's the small stuff — the accessories you add to your existing setup — that make the biggest difference between finishing the workday pain-free and rolling your shoulders for the twentieth time at 3 p.m.

Think about it: you probably already have a desk and a chair. Maybe you even have a decent monitor. But if your screen is sitting on the stock stand it shipped with, your wrists are resting on the hard edge of your desk, and your feet are dangling because your chair's too high — yeah, you're going to feel that by Thursday.

Ergonomic accessories bridge the gap between the setup you have and the setup you need. They're the low-hanging fruit of workspace health. And the best part? Most of them cost less than a nice dinner out.

This guide breaks down every accessory that's worth your money — what it does, who needs it, how much to spend, and which specific products to buy. I've organized it so you can read straight through or jump to the section that's bothering you right now.

Monitor Arms — The #1 Upgrade You Can Make

If you can only buy one ergonomic accessory this year, make it a monitor arm. Full stop.

Here's why: the stand that came with your monitor was designed to get the box checked, not to put the screen at eye level. Most stock stands sit the screen so low you have to tilt your head down to see it. Do that for eight hours a day, and your neck is going to let you know about it.

A monitor arm frees up desk space (no more giant plastic base), lets you position the screen exactly where it needs to be — at eye level, an arm's length away — and makes it trivial to switch between sitting and standing without hunting for a ream of paper to prop things up.

The budget pick: Amazon Basics Monitor Arm ($22)

The Amazon Basics Single Monitor Arm is the best value in ergonomics, full stop. It's a rebadge of a proven design (it shares DNA with the much more expensive Ergotron LX), and at $22, it's almost suspiciously cheap for what it does. It supports monitors up to 32 inches and about 22 pounds, has full articulation (height, tilt, swivel, rotation), and clamps to your desk or mounts through a grommet hole. The gas spring is smooth enough for most users. The only catch? The cable management channels are mediocre, and the plastic joints don't feel quite as premium as pricier options. But if you're on a tight budget, this is the arm to get.

The premium pick: Ergotron LX ($139)

The Ergotron LX is the gold standard for a reason. It's built like a tank — all aluminum, no plastic nonsense. The gas spring is butter-smooth, holding position exactly where you leave it without any drift. It supports up to 20 pounds (most monitors 24 inches and under are fine, though some 27-inch ultrawides push the limit), and the cable management is genuinely useful, not an afterthought. The LX has been the go-to arm for IT departments and ergonomic consultants for over a decade. The price stings at $139, but this is a buy-it-for-life product. I've seen LXs in active use for over a decade without any sag or failure.

Quick buying advice: Make sure your desk has a solid edge for clamping — glass tops and thin particleboard desks can be problematic. Measure your monitor's VESA pattern (most modern monitors are 100×100mm, but it's worth checking before you buy). And if you're running dual monitors, buy two single arms rather than a dual-arm setup — it gives you more flexibility to position each screen independently.

Footrests — Your Feet Aren't Supposed to Dangle

Here's a scenario I see all the time: someone buys an ergonomic chair, adjusts the seat height so their thighs are parallel to the floor — and now their feet don't touch the ground. They sit there with their heels hooked on the chair legs or their toes barely grazing the carpet. It's uncomfortable, but they figure it's fine.

It's not fine.

When your feet aren't supported, your hamstrings tighten, your pelvis tilts backward, and you end up slouching. The whole point of that expensive chair adjustment is lost because there's nothing under your feet. A footrest is the fix.

Who needs one? Anyone who's over about 5'8" in a standard office chair. Anyone using a thick cushion or an aftermarket seat pad. Anyone with a standing-height desk who can't lower it all the way. And honestly, even if your feet do touch the floor, a footrest with a tilt function can make a noticeable difference in lower back comfort by encouraging micro-movements and keeping your legs from going completely static.

The pick: Kensington Solemate Plus ($45)

The Kensington Solemate Plus is the footrest that keeps showing up on every "best of" list for a reason. It's got a wide, stable platform with a textured surface that keeps your feet from sliding around, and the tilt range is generous enough that you can switch angles throughout the day. The foam core is firm without being hard, and the fabric cover is removable and washable — which matters more than you'd think. At $45, it's not the cheapest option, but it's the one that'll last. Cheaper foam footrests tend to flatten out within six months. This one holds up.

If $45 feels steep, the Humanscale FM500 is a solid alternative around $30 — though it's smaller and has a fixed angle, so your range of motion is more limited.

Keyboard Trays — When Your Desk is Too High

Here's a hard truth: most desks are built for writing, not typing. Standard desk height is around 29-30 inches. For comfortable typing, your elbows should be at roughly 90 degrees with your forearms parallel to the floor. Unless you're over 6 feet tall, that means your desk needs to be lower than it is.

You can't easily lower a desk. But you can add a keyboard tray that brings your typing surface down to the right height.

When do you actually need one?

Raise your chair so your thighs are parallel to the floor and your feet are flat. Now rest your hands on your keyboard. If your elbows are below 90 degrees (pointed down toward the floor) or your shoulders are hunched up toward your ears, your desk is too high. A keyboard tray fixes this.

The pick: Vivo Height-Adjustable Keyboard Tray ($39)

The Vivo Height-Adjustable Keyboard Tray is the sweet spot of price and functionality. It clamps under your desk (no drilling required), adjusts for height and tilt angle, and has enough surface area for a full-size keyboard plus a mouse. The slide mechanism is smooth with ball-bearing tracks, and there's a built-in wrist rest (more on those in a minute).

The premium pick: Humanscale Float Keyboard Tray ($229)

The Humanscale Float is what you get when you want the best. It uses a counterbalance mechanism instead of springs or friction knobs, which means it adjusts effortlessly as you move between sitting and standing. It's sleek, takes up minimal lap room, and comes with a curved palm support. The price hurts, but if you have a standing desk and spend serious hours typing, it's a legitimate quality-of-life improvement. The Vivo will do the job for most people. The Float is for the person who's done compromising.

One thing nobody tells you: A keyboard tray reduces your mouse space. If you're a gamer or use design software with a lot of mouse travel, consider a mouse-only tray extension or just keep the mouse on your desk surface while the keyboard goes underneath.

Wrist Rests — Do They Actually Help?

Short answer: yes, but not for the reason most people think.

Wrist rests aren't meant to be something you lean on while you type. If you're resting your full wrist weight on a pad while your fingers fly across the keyboard, you're compressing the carpal tunnel — which is exactly the opposite of what you want. The real purpose of a wrist rest is to serve as a height reference and gentle landing pad — a reminder to keep your wrists straight and a soft spot for your palms to rest between typing bursts.

If you type continuously (like, say, a transcriptionist or a programmer in a flow state), your hands should be floating above the keyboard. The wrist rest is for the micro-breaks in between. If you spend more time reading and clicking than typing (browsing, email, spreadsheets), then yes, a wrist rest can genuinely help by keeping your wrists in a neutral position during those long periods of mousing.

The pick: Glorious Gaming Wrist Pad ($14)

Yes, it says "gaming." Ignore the branding. The Glorious Gaming Stealth Wrist Pad is a dense, memory-foam rest with a smooth, waterproof surface that's easy to clean. It comes in multiple sizes (I'd recommend the full-size for a keyboard or the small for a mouse), and it doesn't flatten out over time like the cheap gel-filled ones do. At $14, it's cheap enough that you can try it without feeling like you've made an investment.

Better than a wrist rest: a floating wrist position

If you can train yourself to type with your wrists straight and your hands hovering, you don't need a wrist rest at all. That's the ideal. A wrist rest is a tool, not a crutch. Use it as a height guide and a rest between sessions, not a platform to press your weight into.

Anti-Fatigue Mats — For Standing Desk Users

If you stand at your desk, you already know that standing on a hard floor for more than 20 minutes starts to ache. Your lower back tightens, your knees get cranky, and before you know it, you're sitting again.

An anti-fatigue mat isn't a gimmick. It works by encouraging micro-movements in your leg muscles — tiny shifts in weight and balance that keep blood flowing and prevent the pooling of blood in your lower legs that makes standing so uncomfortable on hard surfaces. The effect is subtle but real. You'll stand longer, feel less tired, and switch between sitting and standing more often — which is the whole point of having a standing desk in the first place.

The budget pick: Cloudpeak Anti-Fatigue Mat ($33)

The Cloudpeak Anti-Fatigue Mat punches well above its price. It's a 30×20 inch mat with a thick, cushioned foam core and a beveled edge that prevents tripping. The surface texture gives good grip even in socks, and it's available in several colors. At $33, it's cheap enough to buy on a whim and genuinely useful enough to keep. The foam compresses over time — expect about 12-18 months before it starts losing its bounce — but at this price, that's acceptable.

The premium pick: Ergodriven Topo ($119)

The Ergodriven Topo takes a different approach. Instead of just being a cushioned pad, it has a sculpted surface with raised nodes and textured zones designed to encourage active standing — shifting weight, rolling your feet, flexing your calves. It's a much more active experience than standing on a flat foam mat. The hard part is not the foam but the engineered surface design, which is why it costs more. If you stand for more than four hours a day, the Topo is worth the investment. It won't compress or wear out the way foam mats do, and the active standing design genuinely reduces lower back fatigue over a long day.

Mouse Ergonomics — MX Master 3S vs. Trackballs

Your mouse might be the most-used tool on your desk. If it's not comfortable, you'll feel it first in your wrist, then in your forearm, then in your shoulder. It's a chain reaction.

The standard pick: Logitech MX Master 3S ($99)

The Logitech MX Master 3S is the reigning champ of ergonomic mice, and it's popular for good reason. The sculpted shape fills your palm and keeps your hand in a neutral, handshake position rather than the claw-grip that flat mice force you into. The thumb rest is well-placed, the scroll wheel is legendary (it switches between free-spin and notched scrolling), and the silent clicks are genuinely quiet. Battery life is about 70 days on a full charge, and it works across three devices with a button press.

The Kensington Orbit Trackball is the gateway drug to trackball life. Instead of moving the mouse around your desk, you roll a 34mm ball with your thumb or fingers while the base stays in place. The Orbit is wired, has a scroll ring (much nicer than the scroll wheel on cheaper trackballs), and is ambidextrous. At $39, it's an inexpensive way to test whether trackball life works for you. Split Keyboards — The Ergonomic Endgame

A split keyboard is the single biggest shift you can make in your typing posture — and potentially the most impactful ergonomic change after a monitor arm. The idea is simple: instead of a single rigid board that forces your hands together and your wrists to angle outward, a split keyboard lets you position each half shoulder-width apart, keeping your wrists straight and your shoulders relaxed.

The difference is immediate. The first time you type on a split keyboard, you'll notice that your shoulders aren't hunched forward the way they are with a standard keyboard. Your chest opens up. Your wrists don't bend at an angle to reach the home row. It feels right in a way that's hard to describe until you try it.

The pick: Kinesis Freestyle2 ($89)

The Kinesis Freestyle2 is the best entry point for split keyboards. It's not nearly as intimidating as the fully contoured boards you see on r/MechanicalKeyboards — it's essentially two compact keyboard halves connected by a cable, with standard key spacing and a familiar laptop-style scissor-switch feel. No steep learning curve, no weird key placements. You just separate the halves to shoulder width and start typing.

The Freestyle2 comes with a fixed 7-inch separation cable. If you want more separation, Kinesis sells a V3 kit with a 20-inch cable and lifters that angle the keyboard halves for negative tilt — that's another $30 or so. It's worth it if you have broad shoulders or a standing desk.

A note on the transition: Your typing speed will drop for the first week. That's normal. Your brain is rewiring the motor pattern from "hands together" to "hands apart." Stick with it. By day 5 or 6, most people are back to their normal speed, and by week 2, they're faster with less fatigue than they ever were on a standard keyboard.

Under-Desk Cable Management

This one isn't strictly ergonomic — it won't fix your posture or prevent repetitive strain injuries. But cable management matters for your workspace in a way that indirectly affects how often you adjust your setup, how much space you have to move, and how easy it is to switch between sitting and standing.

When your cables are a tangled mess under your desk, you think twice about adjusting your monitor arm or switching your desk height. Those tiny barriers add up over the course of a day. A clean cable setup removes the friction.

The basics:

  • J Channel cable raceway ($13) — Adhesive-backed plastic channel that sticks under your desk. Route all cables through it. Clean, simple, effective.
  • Velcro cable ties ($8) — Reusable and adjustable. Use these instead of zip ties. You'll thank yourself the next time you need to rearrange a cable.
  • Under-desk cable tray ($20) — A mesh tray that bolts or screws into your desk, giving you a shelf to stash power strips, excess cable slack, and adapters.

Total cost to completely clean up a messy desk: about $40. Time investment: one afternoon, most of which is spent unplugging stuff and flipping your desk over. The result: a setup that's noticeably less cluttered and significantly easier to adjust.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I really need a monitor arm, or can I just stack books under my monitor?

You can stack books. But here's what you're losing: the ability to adjust the screen forward or backward, tilt it exactly to reduce glare, swivel it to share with a coworker, and — most importantly — lift it in seconds when you stand up. A monitor arm isn't just about height. It's about adjustability. Books get you one fixed position. An arm gets you every position. That said, if your budget is truly zero, books are better than nothing. Just make sure the stack is stable.

Can I use a gaming mouse for ergonomics?

Some gaming mice have decent shapes (the Logitech G502 has a comfortable thumb shelf, and the Razer DeathAdder V3 has a sculpted right-handed design), but most gaming mice prioritize weight, RGB, and high DPI over sustained comfort. The honeycomb shell designs that save weight are notorious for collecting grime and creating uncomfortable pressure points during long work sessions. If you already have a comfortable gaming mouse, it'll do. But if you're buying new, get an ergonomic mouse first.

Are gel wrist rests better than foam?

Gel wrist rests are cooler to the touch and provide more "sinking in" support, which feels nice initially. But gel compresses and conforms less over time than memory foam, and it doesn't spring back as well. The result: a gel rest might feel great for a month, then start developing permanent divots. Good memory foam (like the Glorious pad) maintains its shape much longer. The exception is gel rests made with medical-grade gel — those are better, and they're priced accordingly. For most people, memory foam wins.

How long does it take to get used to a split keyboard?

Most people adjust within 5-7 days. Your typing speed will drop to about 50-60% of normal on day one. By day three, you'll be around 80%. By day seven, most people are back to their pre-split speed or faster. The real payoff comes at week three or four, when you stop noticing the keyboard entirely — that's when you know your hands are in a neutral position and you're not fighting the hardware anymore. Tip: practice with typing drills for 10 minutes a day during the transition.

I have a standing desk. Do I need a standing mat?

Yes. Even premium standing desks with great ergonomics don't address the floor. Standing on hardwood, tile, or concrete without a mat reduces the amount of time you can comfortably stand by roughly half. The Cloudpeak mat ($33) is a cheap insurance policy that will make you much more likely to actually use your standing desk throughout the day. If you find yourself sitting more than standing, a mat is probably the fix.

What's the single most important purchase for someone with wrist pain?

Two things compete for the top spot. A trackball mouse (like the Kensington Orbit at $39 or the Logitech ERGO M575 at $49) removes the wrist motion that aggravates most repetitive strain issues. But if your pain is keyboard-related — from constantly reaching for the home row with your wrists angled — a split keyboard like the Kinesis Freestyle2 ($89) is more effective. If you can only buy one, start with the trackball. It's cheaper, requires zero learning curve for your keyboarding, and addresses the most common RSI trigger.

Do these accessories work with a standing desk?

Almost all of them do, and several are more valuable with a standing setup. A monitor arm is essential for a standing desk — it lets you keep the screen at eye level whether you're sitting or standing. The anti-fatigue mat is standing-specific. A footrest can still be useful if you use a drafting stool for perching. Cable management becomes more important because the cables need to have enough slack to follow you as you change desk height. The only accessory that's less useful with a standing desk is the keyboard tray — most standing desks go low enough that you don't need one, though the Humanscale Float works beautifully in sit-stand mode.

How often should I replace my ergonomic accessories?

Depends on the item. Monitor arms (Ergotron LX) and trackballs should last 10+ years. Split keyboards typically last 5-7 years with normal use. Foam-based accessories (wrist rests, anti-fatigue mats) need replacing every 1-2 years when they lose their bounce. Footrests with foam cores last about 2-3 years. Cable management is basically permanent. The rule of thumb: if it has foam or padding, it's a consumable. Everything else is an investment.

Is there any harm in using too many ergonomic accessories?

Believe it or not, yes. It's possible to over-engineer your setup. If you have a keyboard tray, a wrist rest, and a gel palm pad on your mouse pad, you might end up with your hands in an awkward position because the cumulative height from all those layers throws off your elbow angle. The goal is neutral posture — straight wrists, elbows at 90 degrees, feet flat. Each accessory should move you toward that goal. If adding something makes you feel more comfortable rather than less, keep it. If it feels off, remove it and reassess. More gear isn't always better gear.

Where should I start if my budget is under $100?

Monitor arm, no question. The Amazon Basics arm at $22 leaves you with nearly $80. Put that toward a Logitech MX Master 3S if your mouse is the weak link, or a Kinesis Freestyle2 split keyboard if typing posture is your priority. Either combination transforms your setup for under $110. If you truly can't stretch past $50, get the monitor arm and a cheap wrist rest, then save for the next upgrade.

Putting It All Together

Here's the thing about ergonomic accessories: they're not magic. No single product is going to fix years of bad posture or undo the effects of sitting eight hours a day. What they will do is remove the obstacles to good posture — the screen that's too low, the desk that's too high, the mouse that forces your wrist into a twist, the floor that makes standing unbearable.

The order matters. Start with the things that touch your body most often: monitor position first, then your pointing device, then your keyboard angle, then your foot support, then everything else. That's the priority chain. Each upgrade makes the next one more effective.

And don't feel like you have to do it all at once. A $22 monitor arm today, a $39 trackball next month, a $45 footrest after that — that's $106 over three months for a setup that will genuinely feel different. I've seen people go from "I can't make it through the afternoon without my neck hurting" to "I don't think about my body while I'm working" with exactly this sequence of upgrades.

That's the goal, right? Stop thinking about your body and just work. Your setup should disappear. Accessories are how you get there.
