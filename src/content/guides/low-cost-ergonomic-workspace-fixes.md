Do not start an ergonomic upgrade by replacing everything. Start with an audit that separates free positioning problems from equipment limitations.

## The five-minute audit

Sit as you normally work and check:

- Are your feet fully supported?
- Can you keep your shoulders relaxed while reaching the keyboard and mouse?
- Are your wrists close to straight rather than bent upward?
- Is the screen high and distant enough that you are not repeatedly leaning forward?
- Is glare making you move into an awkward position?

NIOSH's workstation checklist treats “no” answers as potential problem areas for further investigation. It is a screening tool, not a diagnosis.

## Fixes in priority order

### 1. Reposition what you already own

Move the keyboard and mouse closer. Center the main screen on your body. Change text size before pulling the display too close. Reorient the desk so windows are to the side of the screen rather than directly behind or in front of it.

### 2. Support your feet

If raising the chair helps your arms reach the desk but leaves your feet unsupported, use a stable footrest. Test the idea with a firm, non-slip platform before buying a dedicated product. It should support both feet and allow position changes.

### 3. Separate a laptop screen from its keyboard

A laptop couples screen height and keyboard height. For longer sessions, an external keyboard and mouse let you raise the display while keeping your hands lower. NIOSH specifically recommends independently positionable input devices when possible.

### 4. Raise or move the monitor

A stable riser can improve display height. A monitor arm adds depth and position adjustment when the desk and screen are compatible. Confirm viewing distance and avoid creating a new reach problem for the keyboard.

### 5. Reduce glare before buying brighter lighting

Use blinds, move glossy objects, rotate the workstation, and reduce excessive contrast. OSHA notes that glare and excessive light can contribute to eye fatigue and awkward viewing postures.

## Spend according to the failed check

| Failed check | First intervention |
| --- | --- |
| Feet unsupported | Stable foot support |
| Screen too low | Riser or compatible monitor arm |
| Laptop forces compromise | External keyboard and mouse |
| Keyboard too far away | Clear desk depth and move inputs closer |
| Window glare | Reorient screen or control daylight |

## Recheck after each change

Use one adjustment for several work sessions before stacking more purchases on top. Change positions and take movement breaks even when the setup feels neutral; no single posture should be held continuously.

Persistent or worsening pain, numbness, weakness, or vision problems should not be treated as a shopping problem. Seek appropriate professional advice.
