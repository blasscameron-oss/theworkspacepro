Monitor-arm shopping usually starts with screen size. Desk compatibility is the better first filter. A capable arm is useless when its clamp cannot open around the desktop, its bolt hits a frame rail, or its joints require more depth than the desk and wall provide.

## Measure the mounting zone

Inspect the rear edge from above and below. Record:

1. Total desktop thickness, including any edge lip.
2. Flat underside depth available for the clamp plate.
3. Distance from the rear edge to a frame rail, cable tray, or modesty panel.
4. Clearance behind the desk when it is in its working position.
5. Grommet-hole diameter and underside access, if present.

Compare these measurements with the arm's clamp-opening range and plate dimensions—not only a claim that it “fits most desks.”

## Thick desktops

A thick top needs adequate clamp opening and full contact on both plates. Decorative aprons and beveled edges can make a nominally compatible thickness unusable. Do not attach a clamp to a fragile lip or tighten beyond the desk manufacturer's guidance.

A grommet mount may bypass the edge, but only when the bolt length, hole diameter, and underside plate fit without hitting the frame.

## Shallow desktops

On a shallow desk, the arm needs to place the screen far enough away without forcing the keyboard toward the front edge. NIOSH describes an external display placed roughly an arm's length away, with the top at or below eye level.

Check the arm's folded depth and rear sweep. Some joints extend behind the clamp when the monitor moves backward, which can collide with a wall. A pole-style mount or arm designed to fold over its base may use less rear clearance.

## Match the monitor, not just its diagonal size

Verify the monitor's weight without its original stand, VESA pattern, and any recessed mounting area. Curved and ultrawide monitors can place their center of mass farther from the VESA plate, increasing leverage.

| Compatibility check | Measurement to compare |
| --- | --- |
| Desk edge | Clamp opening and plate footprint |
| Frame clearance | Clamp bolt length and underside space |
| Screen | Supported weight and VESA pattern |
| Wall | Rear sweep in your intended position |
| Viewing position | Forward reach, height, and tilt range |

## Plan cables before tightening anything

Route power and signal cables with enough slack for the arm's full movement. Keep connectors from carrying cable weight, and test the complete range slowly before finalizing ties or sleeves.
